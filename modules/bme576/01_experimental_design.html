Design of Experiments for Model Validation

Multiplexed Cytokine Assay under Cell Stress — Engineering Cancer Course, Module 12

Background: Cell Stress, Cytokines, and Multiplexed ELISA

Tumor cells under stress secrete cytokines

When tumor cells experience stress such as hypoxia (low oxygen) or ionizing radiation, they activate intracellular stress-response pathways (HIF-1α, NF-κB) that upregulate secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Key cytokines include IL-6, TNF-α, IL-8, and VEGF. These secreted factors recruit immune cells to the tumor microenvironment and can promote or suppress anti-tumor immunity depending on concentration and context.

Multiplexed ELISA for cytokine profiling

Platforms such as Luminex (bead-based) and MSD (electrochemiluminescence) allow simultaneous measurement of 10–40+ cytokines from a single well of conditioned media. For this module, we focus on IL-6 concentration (pg/mL) as the primary readout because it is the dominant pro-inflammatory cytokine under combined stress.

The DOE question

We have a mathematical model predicting IL-6 secretion as a function of two stress factors: O₂ level (1–21%) and radiation dose (0–10 Gy). Before running an expensive multiplexed assay, we need to decide: which combinations of O₂ and radiation should we test to efficiently map the cytokine response landscape?

Mathematical model: cytokine secretion under stress

Each stress pathway is modeled as a Hill function. The combined response includes a synergy term α capturing cooperative signaling:

Hypoxia stress signal:

$$S_H = S_{\max,H} \cdot \frac{(21 - O_2)^{n_H}}{(21 - EC_{50,H})^{n_H} + (21 - O_2)^{n_H}}$$

Radiation stress signal:

$$S_R = S_{\max,R} \cdot \frac{D^{n_R}}{EC_{50,R}^{n_R} + D^{n_R}}$$

Combined IL-6 secretion:

$$[\text{IL-6}] = \text{baseline} + (S_H + S_R + \alpha \cdot S_H \cdot S_R) \times \text{scale}$$

Sample calculation: At O₂ = 5% and radiation = 4 Gy with default parameters:

Hypoxia level = 21 - 5 = 16. Half-max hypoxia = 21 - 11 = 10.

\(S_H = 0.9 \times \frac{16^{1.5}}{10^{1.5} + 16^{1.5}} = 0.9 \times \frac{64}{31.6 + 64} = 0.9 \times 0.669 = 0.603\)

\(S_R = 0.85 \times \frac{4^{1.2}}{3^{1.2} + 4^{1.2}} = 0.85 \times \frac{5.28}{3.74 + 5.28} = 0.85 \times 0.585 = 0.497\)

Combined = 0.603 + 0.497 + 0.15 × 0.603 × 0.497 = 1.145

IL-6 = 50 + 1.145 × 500 = 622.5 pg/mL

Background: Full Factorial Designs

What is a full factorial?

A full factorial design tests every combination of factor levels. It guarantees estimation of all main effects and all interactions. The cost is that runs grow as the product of all level counts:

$$N = \prod_{i=1}^{k} L_i$$

where \(k\) is the number of factors and \(L_i\) is the number of levels for factor \(i\).

With replicates: \(N_{\text{total}} = r \cdot \prod L_i + n_{\text{controls}}\)

Cytokine screening budget: You want to test 4 O₂ levels × 4 radiation doses with 3 replicates and 6 controls.

  • Unique conditions: \(4 \times 4 = 16\)
  • Total wells: \(3 \times 16 + 6 = 54\) — fits on one 96-well plate.
  • But add a third factor (e.g., 3 exposure times): \(4 \times 4 \times 3 = 48\) conditions → \(3 \times 48 + 6 = 150\) wells. Two plates needed!

Background: Fractional Factorial Designs

The curse of dimensionality in factorial designs

A full factorial with \(k\) factors at 2 levels requires \(2^k\) runs. This grows fast: 3 factors = 8 runs, 5 factors = 32, 7 factors = 128. Fractional factorials strategically select a subset that preserves the most important information while cutting the run count by half, a quarter, or more.

Notation and construction: \(2^{k-p}\)

The superscript tells the story: \(k\) factors, each at 2 levels, with the design size cut \(p\) times in half. So \(2^{5-2} = 2^3 = 8\) runs test 5 factors in only 8 experiments instead of 32.

To construct the fraction, we choose generators that define later factors as products of earlier ones. For \(2^{3-1}\), we set \(C = AB\) — we only run conditions where the level of C equals the product of A and B.

Generator: \(C = AB\)

Defining relation: Multiply both sides by C: \(I = ABC\)

The defining relation tells us which effects are aliased (confounded). To find the alias of any effect, multiply it by the defining relation:

\(A \times ABC = A^2BC = BC\) → so \(A\) is aliased with \(BC\)

\(B \times ABC = AB^2C = AC\) → so \(B\) is aliased with \(AC\)

\(C \times ABC = ABC^2 = AB\) → so \(C\) is aliased with \(AB\)

Resolution: how severe is the aliasing?

Resolution is the length of the shortest word in the defining relation. It determines which types of effects are confounded:

ResolutionShortest WordAliasing PatternWhat You Can Estimate
III3 letters (e.g., ABC)Main effects aliased with 2-factor interactionsMain effects only (if interactions are negligible)
IV4 letters (e.g., ABCD)Main effects clear; 2FIs aliased with other 2FIsAll main effects cleanly; interactions with caution
V5 letters (e.g., ABCDE)Main effects and 2FIs all clearAll main effects and all 2-factor interactions

The sparsity-of-effects principle

Fractional designs work because of an empirical regularity: in most systems, lower-order effects dominate. Main effects are usually larger than 2-factor interactions, which are larger than 3-factor interactions. If high-order interactions are negligible, confounding them with lower-order effects causes little harm. This is the sparsity-of-effects principle that justifies fractional designs for screening.

However, in cancer biology, synergistic drug interactions (2-factor) are often the key scientific question. Using a Resolution III design when you care about interactions is a common and costly mistake.

Cytokine assay example: You want to screen 5 stress factors: O₂ level, radiation dose, heat shock, nutrient depletion, and acidic pH. A full \(2^5\) design requires 32 conditions. A \(2^{5-2}\) fractional factorial tests all 5 in just 8 conditions — but main effects are aliased with 2-factor interactions (Resolution III).

If you suspect O₂ × radiation interaction is important, this aliasing is problematic. You would need at least Resolution IV (\(2^{5-1}\) = 16 runs) to cleanly separate main effects from all 2-factor interactions.

Why fractions still cover the experimental space well

A key property: a well-chosen fraction projects onto every pair of factors with full coverage. In a \(2^{3-1}\) half-fraction, every 2D projection (A vs B, A vs C, B vs C) still contains all four corner combinations (+/+, +/−, −/+, −/−). The fraction is not random — it is a carefully chosen geometric subset that maintains balance across all factor pairs.

Background: Space-Filling Designs (Latin Hypercube)

Why space-filling?

When factors are continuous, we need designs that fill the space rather than placing points only on a grid. Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) divides each factor range into \(n\) equal strata and places exactly one point in each stratum per factor.

LHS construction: For \(n\) samples in \(d\) dimensions, generate a random permutation \(\pi_j\) of \(\{1, \ldots, n\}\) for each dimension \(j\). Sample \(i\) is:

$$x_{ij} = \frac{\pi_j(i) - U_{ij}}{n}, \quad U_{ij} \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$$

Min nearest-neighbor distance: \(d_{\min} = \min_{i \neq j} \|x_i - x_j\|\)

Discrepancy: largest gap in any 1D marginal projection. Lower = more uniform.

Background: Response Surface Methodology (RSM)

What is RSM?

RSM fits a polynomial surrogate to experimental data, mapping the response landscape and identifying optimal conditions. For our cytokine assay, the "response" is IL-6 concentration and the "optimal" condition is the stress combination that produces peak secretion.

Second-order response surface model (2 factors):

$$\hat{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \beta_{11} x_1^2 + \beta_{22} x_2^2 + \beta_{12} x_1 x_2$$

The \(\beta_{12}\) term captures the interaction between stressors — this corresponds to the synergy parameter in the mechanistic model.

OLS fit: \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{y}\)

Interpreting RSM coefficients: Suppose we fit: \(\hat{y} = 120 + 85 x_1 + 62 x_2 + 12 x_1^2 + 8 x_2^2 + 35 x_1 x_2\)

  • \(\beta_0 = 120\): at center conditions, IL-6 is ~120 pg/mL (moderate secretion)
  • \(\beta_1 = 85\): hypoxia has a stronger effect than radiation (\(\beta_2 = 62\))
  • \(\beta_{12} = 35\): positive interaction — combined stress produces more IL-6 than expected from each stress alone (synergy)
  • Positive curvature terms (\(\beta_{11}, \beta_{22} > 0\)) indicate accelerating response at extremes

Background: Central Composite Design (CCD)

Why CCDs are paired with quadratic models

A central composite design is built specifically to estimate the same second-order model used in response surface methodology. It combines a factorial core for interaction estimation, axial (star) points for curvature, and repeated center points for pure-error estimation.

CCD run count for \(k\) factors:

$$N = 2^k + 2k + n_c$$

For two assay factors (\(k=2\)): \(N = 4 + 4 + n_c\). With \(n_c = 5\), this gives 13 total runs.

Quadratic model supported by CCD:

$$\hat{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \beta_{11} x_1^2 + \beta_{22} x_2^2 + \beta_{12} x_1 x_2$$

Rotatable axial distance: \(\alpha = (2^k)^{1/4}\), so for \(k=2\), \(\alpha = \sqrt{2}\).

Cytokine assay example: In coded units, the 2-factor CCD uses factorial points at \((\pm 1, \pm 1)\), axial points at \((\pm \sqrt{2}, 0)\) and \((0, \pm \sqrt{2})\), plus center replicates at \((0,0)\).

Mapped onto the assay space, the center is O₂ = 11% and dose = 5 Gy. The star points touch the assay boundaries, while the interior factorial square helps estimate interaction terms without spending every run at an extreme condition.

Background: Design Comparison & Selection

No universally "best" design

The right choice depends on your goal: screening broadly, fitting a specific model, or discriminating between competing models?

  • Full Factorial: Estimates ALL interactions. Expensive. Regular grid.
  • Latin Hypercube: Broad exploration of continuous space. Space-filling.
  • Central Composite: Optimal for fitting quadratic response surfaces. Efficient.

D-optimality: A design is D-optimal if it maximizes \(\det(\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})\), minimizing the generalized variance of parameter estimates.

Prediction variance: \(\text{Var}(\hat{y}(\mathbf{x}_0)) = \sigma^2 \, \mathbf{x}_0^T (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{x}_0\)

Synthesis: Choosing the Right DOE for a Cytokine Screening Study

From individual methods to a complete experimental plan

You now know factorial, fractional factorial, LHS, CCD, and response surface designs individually. The final challenge is choosing the right design for your specific study, balancing biological goals, plate constraints, replicate needs, and the statistical analysis you intend to run. There is no universally best design — the answer depends on your question.

The well-budget equation

$$N_{\text{total}} = n_{\text{design runs}} \times n_{\text{cytokines}} \times n_{\text{replicates}} + n_{\text{controls}}$$

A 13-run CCD with 3 cytokines and 3 replicates already needs 123 wells (plus controls) — more than one 96-well plate. The well equation forces hard tradeoffs between design complexity, number of analytes, and replication.

What any single experiment can and cannot do

  • You can: compare cytokine-specific response patterns across a shared O₂×dose design, estimate the model class the DOE supports, and rank conditions for follow-up validation.
  • You cannot: estimate within-well cytokine covariance if each cytokine is measured from a different well (multiplexed platforms like Luminex solve this), recover time-course dynamics without allocating wells to separate harvest times, or infer effects the design was never built to resolve (aliased interactions, curvature in a screening design, unmeasured factors).

A practical workflow for design selection

  1. Count your wells: How many plates can you run? Subtract controls. Divide by (cytokines × replicates). That’s your maximum unique conditions.
  2. State your question: Screening, interaction, or optimization? This determines the minimum design resolution.
  3. Pick the richest feasible design: Use the planner below. If your ideal design doesn’t fit, the tool shows what you must sacrifice (fewer replicates, fewer cytokines, or a simpler design).
  4. Validate with the radar chart: Compare your choice against alternatives on coverage, interaction estimation, curvature detection, cost, and noise robustness.
